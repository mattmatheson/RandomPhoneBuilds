METER — Visual Language

Music Video | April 11–12, 2026 | Shared Reference for Crew

This document defines the visual targets for both shoot days. All crew should review before shoot day.

Section 1

The Concept — Two Worlds

Day 1 — The Subway, Nashville (Night Shoot): Cold, dark, teal/cyan institutional. The look is built entirely from Baxter's "Television" aesthetic. Think corridor fluorescents, metal surfaces, ghostly skin, deep blue shadows. Everything is cold.

Day 2 — Cortney & Luke's House: Warm, amber, intimate living room. The Linkin Park "Papercut" video is the template — table lamps, wallpaper, leather furniture, amber everything. The warmth is the antidote to Day 1.

The contrast between cold and warm IS the video's visual thesis. We intercut between the two worlds — the loneliness of the subway against the intimacy of the house. The overall mood sits squarely in the early 2000s dark alternative space. Think Evanescence era. Y2K but not playful — brooding, textured, atmospheric.

Day 1 Lighting Notes for Paul

  • Paul's tube set (8 tubes) mounted along subway car walls, set to CYAN/TEAL
  • FLICKERING — Matt specifically wants unsettling flicker on the tubes
  • FOG/HAZE is critical — catches the tube light and creates atmospheric volume
  • Keep it DARK. Don't fill shadows. Let faces go to silhouette.
  • No power on site — all generator powered (Matt's 3500W + Paul's van generator)
  • Shoot window: sunset to ~midnight (~6 hours)

Section 5

Transition & Cutaway Language

  • Whip pan motion blur — shoot intentional whip pans between subjects as transitions
  • Flash cuts between worlds — cold subway footage cut into warm house and vice versa
  • ECU of faces — profiles, jawlines, eyes, mouths. Fill the entire frame.
  • ECU of hands — on instruments, surfaces, objects
  • Detail/texture shots — tile, metal, fabric, glass, faucets, pipes, screws
  • Face against glass — ghostly, frosted, eerie
  • Intentional motion blur abstracts — shoot some pure-blur frames for edit use
  • The cold-to-warm intercut is the visual thesis of the entire piece
