That's Life

A look back at Foundation Skateboards' landmark video — a raw, punk-fueled full-length that captured the chaotic energy of early 2000s skateboarding and launched one of the most unhinged careers the sport has ever seen.

The Video

"That's Life" dropped in 2004, and it landed like a Molotov cocktail on the front porch of polished skate media. While other companies were chasing cinematic production values and pristine HD footage, Foundation put out a video that felt like it was filmed in the back of a van that smelled like cigarettes and warm beer. And that was entirely the point.

Directed and produced by Tod Swank — Foundation's founder and the quiet architect behind decades of skateboarding's weirdest and most authentic moments — "That's Life" was a full-length team video that showcased Foundation's roster at a moment when the company was hitting a creative stride. The skating was gnarly, the editing was fast, and the whole thing had an energy that felt genuinely dangerous.

Foundation never tried to be the biggest company. They tried to be the realest. "That's Life" is what happens when a brand that doesn't care about marketability just points a camera at its team and lets the chaos speak for itself.

Foundation Skateboards & Tod Swank

The Company

Foundation Skateboards was started by Tod Swank in 1989. Swank had been a pro skater himself — riding for companies like Vision and SMA in the 1980s — and transitioned into the business side with a specific vision: build a company that prioritized the weird, the raw, and the unpolished. Foundation was never about the most technically gifted skaters or the cleanest video parts. It was about personality, creativity, and an attitude that said "we don't care if you like us."

The company operated under the Tum Yeto distribution umbrella alongside Toy Machine, Pig Wheels, Dekline Footwear, and others — a constellation of brands that shared a warehouse, a punk rock ethos, and a general disregard for corporate skateboarding.

Tod Swank

Swank is one of skateboarding's most important behind-the-scenes figures. As a filmer, editor, team manager, and company owner, he shaped the careers of dozens of skaters while maintaining an almost stubborn commitment to keeping things raw. He filmed on Hi8 and MiniDV when others were upgrading to HD. He edited with a style that favored energy over polish. He picked riders based on personality as much as skill. The result was a catalog of videos that feel more like punk zines than corporate product — and "That's Life" is arguably the peak of that philosophy.

The Riders

The "That's Life" roster was a mix of established Foundation veterans and newer additions who were about to blow up. The team at the time included a crew of skaters who all shared that Foundation DNA — a willingness to throw themselves down things that most people would walk away from.

Breakout Part

Corey Duffel

The teenager who stole the entire video. Reckless speed, massive gaps, and a style that looked like he was actively trying to destroy himself. Instantly iconic.

The Corey Duffel Part

If "That's Life" is remembered for one thing, it's Corey Duffel's part. Duffel was a teenager when the video came out, and his section immediately became one of the most talked-about video parts of the year. It wasn't just the tricks — it was the sheer velocity and apparent disregard for personal safety that made people rewind the tape.

Self-Preservation

Speed as a Weapon

Duffel's defining characteristic was speed. While most street skaters approach obstacles with measured, controlled pushes, Duffel looked like he was fleeing something. He bombed into gaps and stair sets with a full-sprint approach that added a visceral, almost nauseating intensity to every trick. The speed wasn't reckless for its own sake — it was how he generated the pop and distance for the massive gaps he was clearing. But it made every clip feel like it could end in catastrophe.

The Music

Duffel's part was set to AFI — fitting for a skater who looked like he'd walked straight out of a horror-punk show. With his long black hair, skeletal frame, and all-black wardrobe, Duffel's aesthetic matched the music perfectly. It wasn't just a soundtrack choice; it was world-building. The part felt like a complete artistic statement, with the dark, driving music amplifying the intensity of the skating.

The Aesthetic

Filming & Editing

Tod Swank's approach to filming and editing was the anti-thesis of what mainstream skate videos were becoming in the mid-2000s. While companies like Girl and Transworld were pushing toward cinematic production — dolly shots, color grading, elaborate B-roll — Foundation stuck with a raw, handheld, VX1000-era feel. The fisheye lens was front and center. The long-lens angles were functional, not artistic. The editing was fast and punchy, matching the energy of the skating rather than trying to aestheticize it.

There were no luxury hotel montages. No trips to Barcelona with first-class flights. Foundation's skaters were filmed at crusty spots in industrial parks, cracked schoolyard lots, and whatever downtown ledge hadn't been skate-stopped yet. The locations matched the energy: rough, unglamorous, and real.

The Soundtrack

"That's Life" was wall-to-wall punk, metal, and hardcore. The music choices were aggressive and fast — perfectly synced to the pace of the skating. This wasn't a video where you'd hear indie folk or jazz over a tech ledge part. Every song selection reinforced the identity: Foundation is loud, Foundation is fast, Foundation doesn't care if you think it's too much.

The Foundation Identity

By 2004, Foundation had carved out a niche that no other company really occupied. They weren't the tech brand (that was Girl/Chocolate). They weren't the art brand (that was Alien Workshop). They weren't the party brand (that was Baker). Foundation was the weird-punk-DIY brand — the company for skaters who were too strange for the mainstream but too committed to be dismissed. "That's Life" was the purest expression of that identity put to tape.

Why It Still Matters

Corey Duffel's Career

Duffel's part in "That's Life" launched him into immediate prominence. He went on to have a long career in professional skateboarding, riding for Foundation for years and becoming one of the brand's most recognizable figures. His skating evolved — he became more technical over time, added transition skating to his repertoire, and continued pushing himself into progressively gnarlier terrain. But it all started here, with a teenager bombing full-speed into stair sets while AFI screamed in the background.

Duffel also became known for his resilience. He suffered serious injuries throughout his career — including a devastating car accident that many thought would end his skating — but kept coming back. That refusal to quit was already visible in "That's Life," in every slam clip that preceded a make.

The Video's Influence

"That's Life" demonstrated that you didn't need a massive budget to make a skate video that people would remember. In an era when the industry was trending toward expensive, highly-produced full-lengths, Foundation proved that energy, personality, and raw skating could carry a video further than production value alone. It was a reminder that skateboarding's power comes from the streets, not the edit suite.

The video also reinforced Foundation's position as a feeder system for skating culture — a brand that found interesting people, gave them boards, pointed a camera at them, and let the results speak for themselves. That model influenced how smaller brands approached team-building and video production for years afterward.

The Tum Yeto Ecosystem

Foundation didn't exist in a vacuum. As part of the Tum Yeto family — alongside Ed Templeton's Toy Machine, another brand famous for raw, personality-driven videos — the company was part of a distribution network that proved independent skateboarding could sustain itself without corporate backing. "That's Life" was a product of that ecosystem: modest budget, total creative freedom, no marketing department telling anyone to tone it down.
