Five Ways to Brew Better Coffee

A quick-reference guide to the most popular manual brewing methods and what makes each one unique.

There's no single "best" way to brew coffee — it depends on what you're after. Here's a breakdown of five popular methods, how they work, and what kind of cup they produce.

01

Pour Over

Clean Cup

The pour over is all about clarity. Hot water passes through a paper filter and a bed of ground coffee by gravity alone, producing a bright, nuanced cup that highlights origin flavors. It rewards patience and precision.

The ritual of a slow, circular pour is part of the appeal. Devices like the V60, Chemex, and Kalita Wave each produce slightly different results based on filter shape and flow rate.

02

French Press

Full Body

The French press is the no-fuss classic. Coarse grounds steep in hot water for four minutes, then a metal mesh plunger separates the brew. No paper filter means all the oils and fine particles end up in your cup.

The result is rich, heavy, and full-bodied — almost velvety. It's forgiving on technique and great for brewing multiple cups at once. Just don't let it sit on the grounds too long or it'll over-extract.

03

AeroPress

Versatile

The AeroPress is a cult favorite for good reason. It's a small plastic plunger that uses air pressure to push water through a compact puck of coffee. Portable, fast, and nearly impossible to break.

What makes it special is its versatility — you can brew something espresso-like or something closer to a pour over just by changing the grind, water temp, and steep time. There's even an annual championship dedicated to finding the best recipe.

04

Moka Pot

Intense

The stovetop Moka pot brews by pushing steam-pressurized water up through finely ground coffee. It originated in Italy in the 1930s and remains a staple in European kitchens.

The result is a strong, concentrated brew — not true espresso, but closer to it than most home methods. It pairs beautifully with milk and makes a solid base for lattes or Americano-style drinks.

05

Cold Brew

Smooth

Cold brew takes the long road. Coarse grounds steep in cold or room-temp water for 12 to 24 hours, then the mixture is filtered. No heat means lower acidity and a naturally sweeter, smoother flavor profile.

The concentrate you get is strong — most people dilute it 1:1 with water or milk. It keeps in the fridge for up to two weeks, making it a great batch-prep option for iced coffee all week.
