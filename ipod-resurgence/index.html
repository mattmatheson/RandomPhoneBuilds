The iPod Resurgence

Why the 5th gen Wolfson DAC is the best-sounding pocket device ever made — and why thousands of people are buying them again.

Apple discontinued the iPod in 2022. And then something weird happened — people started buying them again. Not out of pure nostalgia, but because the 5th generation iPod, with its Wolfson WM8758 DAC, produces audio quality that nothing in your pocket today can touch. Here's everything you need to know.

01

The Revival Is Real

Trend

This isn't a handful of Reddit nerds. eBay searches for the iPod Classic jumped 25% between January and October 2025 compared to the year before. Searches for "iPod Classic" on resale platforms like Wallapop surged 73% since 2022. Used iPod sales grew 15.6% annually in 2024, with prices ranging from $50 to $350 depending on model and condition.

Gen Z is driving a lot of this. They're calling it "friction-maxxing" — intentionally choosing slower, more deliberate tech over algorithmic convenience. As Libby Rodney, chief strategy officer at The Harris Poll, puts it: "We're moving away from total, seamless, convenience culture and back to finding meaning in friction."

The logic is simple. An iPod does one thing: play music. No notifications. No doom-scrolling. No algorithm deciding what you should hear next. You load your music, you press play, and for the first time in years you actually listen to a full album front-to-back.

YouTube creator DankPods — now at 1.8 million subscribers — essentially reignited the entire iPod modding scene when his channel exploded during COVID in 2020, going from 2,000 subscribers to over a million in under a year. His videos showing massive storage builds and sound comparisons introduced a whole new generation to what these devices could do.

02

The Wolfson WM8758

The Chip

Here's why audiophiles lose their minds over the 5th and 5.5th generation iPod: it uses the Wolfson WM8758 digital-to-analog converter. This is the chip that takes your digital music file and turns it into an analog signal that drives your headphones. And this particular chip does it beautifully.

The Wolfson WM8758 is a stereo DAC with an integrated headphone amplifier — meaning the entire analog audio path from digital conversion to headphone output is handled by a single, purpose-built chip. The sound signature is consistently described as warm, full, and musical. Bass has real weight to it. Mids are rich and present. The high end is smooth without being rolled off.

"I discovered it had the Wolfson chip after the sound blew me away. I was running Rockbox playing lossless FLACs and couldn't believe this was coming from an iPod."

— Head-Fi forum member

The specs alone don't tell the full story. The Wolfson has what people describe as "soul" — an analog-like quality to the sound that makes digital music feel less clinical and more like you're hearing it through proper hi-fi gear. This is partly down to the chip's architecture and partly down to how it handles the analog output stage.

What Apple Changed (And Why People Were Mad)

Starting with the 6th generation iPod Classic in 2007, Apple switched to Cirrus Logic CS42L55 DACs. Apple claimed the Cirrus chips had lower distortion numbers. Technically, that's true — the Cirrus measures flatter and has better crosstalk performance on paper.

But measurement isn't everything. The audiophile community has been arguing about this for nearly two decades, and the consensus from people who actually listen is clear: the Wolfson sounds deeper, with more bass presence, and overall the sound has more dimension to it. The Cirrus chips sound accurate but flat — technically correct in a way that strips the musicality out.

Apple eventually acquired Cirrus Logic in a deeper partnership, and every iPhone, iPad, and iPod after the 5.5G used their chips. The Wolfson era was over.

03

5th Gen vs. 5.5 Gen

Know the Difference

Both the 5th gen and 5.5 gen iPods use the Wolfson WM8758 and share the same model number (A1136), which makes them tricky to tell apart. But the 5.5 gen — officially called the "Enhanced" — has meaningful upgrades.

5th Generation (2005)

  • Wolfson WM8758 DAC
  • 30 GB or 60 GB HDD
  • Standard LCD brightness
  • No search function
  • Model: A1136

5.5 Gen "Enhanced" (2006)

  • Wolfson WM8758 DAC
  • 30 GB or 80 GB HDD
  • Brighter, sharper LCD
  • Search function added
  • Model: A1136 (same!)

The quickest way to identify a 5.5 gen: check the main menu. If it has "Search" — it's the Enhanced model. The 5.5 gen also has a noticeably brighter screen and came in an 80 GB option (vs. 60 GB for the 5th gen's larger model) thanks to a thinner single-platter hard drive.

Both sound essentially identical through the headphone jack since they share the same DAC and analog output stage. The 5.5 gen is generally more sought-after because it's the later revision with the screen and storage improvements, but either one gives you the Wolfson sound.

04

Lossless Through a Real DAC

Audio

The iPod 5/5.5G natively supports Apple Lossless (ALAC) — no hacks required. You can sync lossless files straight from iTunes. But the real unlock is installing Rockbox, the open-source firmware, which adds support for FLAC, WAV, Ogg Vorbis, and basically every audio format that exists.

Here's what happens when you feed an uncompressed or lossless file to the Wolfson WM8758: you hear everything. No lossy compression artifacts. No Bluetooth codec stripping away detail. No cheap phone DAC cutting corners. The full dynamic range of the recording, converted by a chip that was designed specifically to make music sound good, pushed through a dedicated headphone amplifier straight to your ears.

Plug in a decent pair of wired headphones — something like the Sennheiser HD 25s or even a pair of good IEMs — and the difference between this and your phone is immediately obvious. The soundstage is wider. Instruments have separation. Bass notes have texture instead of just being loud. Vocals sit in the mix instead of floating in front of everything else.

It sounds like the difference between watching a movie on your phone vs. a proper TV. Same content, completely different experience.

The headphone jack outputs a clean signal with enough power to drive most headphones without a separate amp. This is something phones gave up years ago — even the ones that still have a headphone jack use cheap, low-power DACs that can barely drive anything over 32 ohms.

05

Why Your Phone Can't Do This

The Problem

The headphone jack is gone from most phones. Apple killed it in 2016 with the iPhone 7. Samsung followed. Google followed. Now you're listening through Bluetooth, and Bluetooth has a fundamental problem: compression.

The Bluetooth Bottleneck

Every Bluetooth audio connection compresses your audio before transmitting it. Even "high quality" codecs throw data away:

SBC
328 kbps max
AAC
256 kbps
LDAC
990 kbps max
Lossless CD
1,411 kbps

Even LDAC at its best (990 kbps) is still lossy — and it frequently drops to 330 kbps in real-world use to maintain connection stability. You're paying for Apple Music Lossless or Tidal HiFi and then throwing away most of the data before it reaches your ears.

Cost-Optimized Phone DACs

The few phones that still have headphone jacks use DACs that are afterthoughts. Smartphone manufacturers allocate their component budget to cameras, displays, and processors. The audio DAC and amplifier are bottom-of-the-bill parts, designed to be "good enough" — not good. They share power rails with noisy cellular radios, run at minimal power to save battery, and often can't drive anything beyond basic earbuds.

The iPod 5.5G, by contrast, is a dedicated music player. Its entire signal path — from storage to DAC to amplifier to headphone jack — was designed for one purpose: making music sound as good as possible. It has no cellular radio creating interference, no background apps fighting for processing power, and a Wolfson chip that was premium even by 2006 standards.

06

The Modding Scene

Build Yours

The best part about the iPod 5/5.5G in 2026: you can buy one for around $30–60 on eBay, then mod it into something better than it ever was new. The modding community has made every component replaceable and upgradeable.

iFlash Storage

The original spinning hard drive is the first thing to go. The iFlash adapter replaces the HDD ribbon cable with a microSD slot (or four, with the iFlash Quad). You can drop in a 512 GB or even 1 TB microSD card — that's enough for your entire lossless music library. The iPod runs faster, quieter, lighter, and uses less battery with flash storage.

Battery Upgrades

The original battery is almost certainly dead at this point. Drop-in replacement batteries are available from $8–15 and range from 1,200 mAh (stock equivalent) up to 3,000 mAh thick batteries that turn the 30 GB model's thin case into a battery monster. With flash storage and a big battery, you can get 40–60+ hours of playback.

Rockbox Firmware

Rockbox is a free, open-source replacement firmware that transforms the iPod. It adds FLAC support, a parametric equalizer, gapless playback, custom themes, and — critically — removes the need for iTunes entirely. Drag and drop your music. It also tends to extract slightly better sound quality from the Wolfson DAC thanks to its direct hardware access.

Cosmetic Mods

New faceplates, click wheels, back panels (including transparent ones), and colored headphone jacks are all available from suppliers like Elite Obsolete Electronics and iDemiGods. You can build a completely unique iPod that looks brand new.

07

The Bottom Line

TL;DR

The iPod 5th/5.5th generation with the Wolfson WM8758 DAC is the best-sounding portable music device most people can afford. Not because it has the flattest frequency response or the lowest measured distortion — but because it was designed by people who cared about how music actually sounds through headphones.

Modern phones optimized the headphone jack out of existence. Bluetooth compresses everything. Streaming services made us passive listeners. The modded iPod is the antidote: a device that does one thing, does it exceptionally well, and asks you to be intentional about what you listen to.

Grab a 5.5 gen off eBay. Buy an iFlash adapter and a fat battery. Install Rockbox. Load it with lossless music. Plug in real headphones. Press play.

You'll hear the difference in about three seconds.
