Biography
From Funeral Homes
to Stadiums

Dan Spencer was raised on gospel quartets and John Denver in Smyrna, Tennessee — about an hour from Nashville. At age 11, he discovered Slipknot's Vol. 3 and spent the rest of his adolescence chasing the heaviest music he could find through the pages of Terrorizer Magazine.

He spent his twenties in bands — Holy Coast, Pumpkinseed — touring the DIY circuit. When the band broke up, he enrolled in mortuary school in Nashville, studying both embalming and funeral directing while working full-time at a funeral home. He learned to suture, cremate, apply cosmetics to the dead, and pick up bodies from homes at 3 AM.

The mortuary shaped everything. "Once you kind of get over the idea of 'I'm not hurting this person,' it's — after a while there's nothing to it," he told the Maximum Zach podcast. The regional idioms of older community members, the dark humor of the profession, and the raw confrontation with mortality all poured into his songwriting.

He started recording solo material during the pandemic, shelved it, then watched it attract label attention. He dropped out of mortuary school with one semester remaining. The connection that changed everything? A regular customer at the pizza restaurant he managed in Nashville turned out to be in the music industry. That customer showed Spencer's music to friends. Those friends showed it to Post Malone.

Late-night writing sessions followed — one at a downtown bar around 2 AM with HARDY and ERNEST, then relocating to Post Malone's studio near Belmont. A December session with Brad Paisley. A benefit concert in Shelbyville alongside Jelly Roll, Eddie Vedder, and Post Malone in a 250-person venue. Then the call: opening act for the F-1 Trillion Tour.

On October 19, 2024, Dan Spencer stood on the stage at Nissan Stadium in Nashville — 70,000 seats — opening for Post Malone's first-ever stadium show. From pizza restaurants to stadiums.

He calls his sound "Country Cult Baroque" — a term borrowed from the late Judee Sill. Metal Injection called him "the most metal artist making non-metal music." His album titles are Seinfeld references. He moved to Cookeville because "it's got this pretty unique history of having a way better music scene than it ought to."

He is currently on a 22-city headlining tour and signed to Feeling Grey Records — a joint venture between Post Malone's Electric Feel publishing and Nashville's Grey Area Records.
