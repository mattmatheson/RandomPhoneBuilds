V. Issues with the Staff Response

A. The "Check It In Your Car" Defense

The suggestion that a customer should open every case in the parking lot after a large purchase is absurd. When you buy $200 worth of product from a store, there is an implied trust that the product is complete. You don't buy a pizza and open the box in the restaurant to count the slices. You don't buy a book and flip to the last page to make sure it's there. You don't buy a double feature and pop open the case in the car to verify that the word "double" was used accurately. The product should be what it says it is.

B. The Receipt Situation

Not having a physical receipt should not be a dealbreaker in 2026. The store has a digital transaction system — they proved this by eventually finding the order. The complainant provided photographic evidence with timestamps. The lack of a paper receipt — a piece of thermal paper coated in chemicals that the European Union has literally restricted — should not be held against a customer when digital verification is available.

C. The AI Meltdown

The complainant asked a simple, reasonable question about whether their system had a search function or could use AI to speed up the lookup. This was met with hostility. The staff member's personal feelings about artificial intelligence should not affect customer service. Nobody asked him to become a prompt engineer. Nobody asked him to install ChatGPT. The question was: "Can you search for it faster?" That's it.

The additional irony here is that the complainant went home and had an AI write this entire legal-style grievance document, build it into a website, and publish it to the internet in about four minutes. The AI found the store's address, phone number, Facebook page, and hours faster than the staff found a receipt. So.

D. The Credit vs. The Disc

This is perhaps the most baffling part. The store now has a disc with no case. The complainant has a case with no disc. The obvious, logical, elementary-school-level solution is to reunite them. Instead, the store issued credit, which means:

  • The customer still doesn't have the movie he paid for
  • The store has a loose disc they can't sell
  • Nobody wins
  • This is the opposite of problem solving
  • This would not pass a kindergarten logic test

VII. Summary of Grievances

  1. Product was incomplete at time of sale. A double feature was sold with only one film. This is, by definition, not a double feature.
  2. This is a recurring issue. The complainant has experienced missing discs before and previously absorbed the loss to maintain a good relationship with the store.
  3. Staff was dismissive and unhelpful. The "check it in your car" defense is not customer service. It's blame-shifting.
  4. A customer was profiled based on appearance. The complainant was treated as an outsider despite being one of the store's most loyal and highest-spending customers.
  5. The AI comment triggered an unprofessional response. A customer should not be treated rudely for asking a question about technology. Especially at a technology store.
  6. The resolution was illogical. Store credit instead of the actual disc that both parties know exists somewhere in that building.
  7. A $3,000+ lifetime customer was treated like a suspect trying to pull off a Blu-ray heist. The Hunger Games disc 1 retails for approximately $4. This is not Ocean's Eleven.
