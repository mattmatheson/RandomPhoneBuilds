The Video That Changed Everything

Released in 2002, Sorry was Flip Skateboards' first full-length video — and it landed like a bomb. Directed by Jeremy Fox and filmed primarily by Fred Mortagne (known universally as "French Fred"), the video was shot across the United States, Spain, France, and the United Kingdom over a period of roughly five years.

What set Sorry apart wasn't just the skating — though the skating was absurd. It was the personality. The entire 60-minute film was introduced and interlinked by John Lydon, a.k.a. Johnny Rotten of the Sex Pistols, who delivered deadpan commentary between parts. His most famous line about the team: "As brilliant as they are on their skateboards, they're fucking idiots off them."

The video opened with Nancy Sinatra's "These Boots Are Made For Walking" and The Stooges' "I Wanna Be Your Dog" — immediately setting a tone that was equal parts irreverent and aggressive. Between the banter, the ridiculous quick scenes, and the gnarly skateboarding, Sorry's influence on the skate community was forever cemented.

Sorry "really changed what people thought was possible within skateboarding." The tricks done in this video still stand up to the standards of professional skateboarding today — over two decades later.

Years Filming

The first trick filmed for Sorry was Geoff Rowley's lipslide down the double kink rail at Laguna — shot all the way back in 1997. The last trick filmed was Tom Penny's ledge line at Belmont in Long Beach, captured just days before the May 21, 2002 premiere. Five years of footage, distilled into one hour.

Behind the Camera

Fred Mortagne — "French Fred"

The primary filmmaker behind Sorry, French Fred's cinematography elevated skate video from shaky handheld footage to something approaching art. His use of long-lens filming, creative angles, and the iconic fisheye work became a signature of the Flip aesthetic. He traveled with the team across four countries to capture every part.

Jeremy Fox

Co-director alongside Fred Mortagne and Geoff Rowley, Fox handled much of the editing and post-production. The decision to bring in John Lydon as an on-camera host gave the video a narrative structure that most skate videos of the era completely lacked.

John Lydon — Johnny Rotten

The Sex Pistols frontman served as the video's unlikely narrator, appearing between parts to roast the skaters and introduce their sections. During filming, Lydon opted for real alcohol instead of prop drinks and got progressively drunker as the shoot went on. The crew worried the footage would be unusable — but it turned out better that way. He later wore Sorry t-shirts to high-profile events. His punk credibility gave the video a cultural weight that extended well beyond the skate world.

The Parts

Sorry featured eight full parts from Flip's international roster. Each skater brought a completely different style, making the video feel like a world tour of what skateboarding could be.

Mark Appleyard

Placebo — "Every You Every Me"

The Canadian tech wizard opens the individual parts. Appleyard's smooth, creative lines through street spots made Placebo's track inseparable from his skating — to this day, fans say they can't hear the song without picturing his part. He was 18 during filming and went on to win Thrasher's Skater of the Year in 2003, largely on the strength of his Sorry footage.

Ali Boulala

Gray Matter — "Burn No Bridges"

The wild Swede. Boulala's part is pure chaos — equal parts incredible skating and absurd off-board antics. His hijinks, fearless stair attacks, and the legendary Lyon 25-stair ollie attempt became the most talked-about footage in the entire video. Every second is unpredictable.

Alex Chalmers

Faction — "Let's Go Get Cokes"

The unsung ripper. Chalmers brought raw energy to a no-nonsense hardcore punk soundtrack. His part is fast, aggressive street skating that showed you didn't need to be the most famous name on the team to deliver one of the most watchable sections.

Bastien Salabanzi

Mr. Dibbs — "Side A / Side B Excerpts"

The French prodigy. Salabanzi was already turning heads as a contest destroyer, and his Sorry part confirmed the hype. Technical precision mixed with power — his ability to combine flip tricks with massive gaps was ahead of its time.

Geoff Rowley

Gray Matter — "I Am The Walrus"

The boss. As both a co-director and the team's spiritual leader, Rowley's part is pure commitment — huge drops, terrifying rails, and an intensity that defined what "gnarly" meant in early 2000s skating. His lipslide down the Laguna double kink rail was literally the first trick filmed for the entire video, back in 1997. Thrasher SOTY 2000.

Rune Glifberg

The Prodigy — "Breathe"

The Danish transition king. Glifberg's park and pool footage set to The Prodigy was an adrenaline shot in the middle of a street-heavy video. His fluid, powerful style on vert and in bowls showed the range of Flip's team beyond pure street skating.

Tom Penny

The Velvet Underground — "Venus in Furs"

The enigma. Penny is widely considered one of the most naturally talented skateboarders who ever lived, and his Sorry part — though not his longest — showed why. Effortless style, zero visible effort. His mini-ramp section set to Édith Piaf's "La Vie En Rose" is one of the most memorable sequences in skate video history. His last trick was the final footage captured for the entire project, filmed just days before the premiere. Penny severely injured his ankle near Salvador Dalí's house in Cadaqués, Spain at 6 AM jumping between rocks — his footage had to be rationed carefully between Sorry and éS' Menikmati.

Arto Saari

David Bowie — "1984" / "Rock 'n' Roll Suicide"

The ender. Saari got the coveted last part — the exclamation point on the whole video. Set to David Bowie, his section is an onslaught of heavy street skating with a refined, almost photographic eye for spot selection. Coming off his 2001 Thrasher SOTY win, Saari delivered exactly what a closing part demands: something you can't follow. His filming wasn't without cost — during a warm-up feeble grind, his kingpin caught in a hole in the rail and pitched him to the ground, resulting in a massive concussion, temporary amnesia, a broken hand, and temporary blindness. He was hospitalized for two and a half days. The incident was recreated with animation in the video.

The Lyon 25

No discussion of Sorry is complete without the Lyon 25-stair set — a monstrous staircase in Lyon, France that became skateboarding's Everest.

Ali Boulala attempted to ollie the full 25 stairs on two separate occasions during filming for Sorry. On his first attempt, he actually landed on his board — but the deck exploded on impact. The sheer force of dropping that distance shattered the wood beneath his feet.

The second attempt was do-or-die. It was the last day he could try it — French Fred was leaving for the U.S. the next morning to begin editing the video. On the way to the spot, it started to drizzle. Boulala had to set up a fresh board and, feeling rushed by the weather closing in, went for it without properly warming up.

He landed on his board but couldn't stay on. Then he tried again — but the run-up was getting wet, his foot slipped while pushing at full speed, and he lost his balance at the point of no return.

The Lyon 25 wouldn't be successfully landed until 2015, when Jaws (Aaron Homoki) ollied it clean — thirteen years after Boulala's attempts. The staircase has since become one of the most famous spots in skateboarding history, with Boulala's Sorry footage as its origin story.

Behind the Scenes

Ali Boulala vs. the Car Windshield

In the video's intro, Boulala smashed a car windshield — and was nearly jumped by angry locals as a result. At the Lyon premiere, he attended incognito wearing a hat and glasses to avoid being recognized.

Rune Glifberg's Four-Month Part

Rune Glifberg's entire section was filmed in just four months. Flip owner Jeremy Fox hadn't even expected Glifberg to have a full part — but he stacked enough footage to earn one.

The Skydiving Ending That Never Was

The end credits were supposed to show Geoff Rowley doing a BASE jump. After his first skydive, Rowley realized he could actually die and would need 30–40 more jumps to get the necessary experience. The idea was scrapped and replaced with an animated monkey.

PJ Ladd's Ghost Intro

A Johnny Rotten intro was filmed for PJ Ladd, who wanted to ride for Flip. But Ladd was contractually unable to appear in the video. He'd go on to appear in Really Sorry the following year, delivering one of the most rewatched parts in skate history.

20th Anniversary

In 2022, Flip released a 20th Anniversary deck series reissuing the original Sorry graphics, and the soundtrack was released on vinyl LP — confirming the video's status as a cultural artifact that transcended skateboarding.

Legacy & the Sorry Trilogy

Sorry didn't just establish Flip as a video powerhouse — it redefined what a team video could be. The combination of international filming, A-list soundtrack curation, a punk rock narrator, and career-defining parts from every skater set a new bar that companies spent the rest of the decade trying to reach.

The video's success led to two sequels, forming the "Sorry Trilogy":

2002

Sorry — The original. Directed by Jeremy Fox and Fred Mortagne. Premiered May 21, 2002. Instantly recognized as a classic.

2003

Really Sorry — The follow-up, produced by Ewan Bowman. Added PJ Ladd and Danny Cerezini to the roster. PJ Ladd's part in particular became one of the most rewatched sections in skate video history.

2009

Extremely Sorry — The trilogy closer. Introduced David Gonzalez (2012 SOTY) and Luan Oliveira to the Flip team. Filmed in HD, marking the transition to a new era of skate video production.

Five Flip team members — past and present — have won Thrasher's Skater of the Year award: Bob Burnquist (1997), Geoff Rowley (2000), Arto Saari (2001), Mark Appleyard (2003), and David González (2012). The Sorry era alone produced three consecutive winners.

Sorry is regularly cited alongside videos like Girl's Yeah Right! (2003) and éS' Menikmati (2000) as one of the defining skate videos of the early 2000s golden age. Some fans on Letterboxd have called it "probably the most influential skate video of all time."
